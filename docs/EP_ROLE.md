# The Executive Producer Role

A reference for what an executive producer actually does on a podcast, what part of that role a solo creator can hand to software, and where human judgment still has to sit in the chair.

## The role in one paragraph

An executive producer owns the show. They set editorial direction, defend the format, direct the talent, run the calendar, sign off on quality, and read the numbers after a release to decide what to do next. A producer executes — books, records, edits, ships. An EP decides what the show is, what it covers, who is on it, and whether last week's episode worked. On a network show, those are two people. On a solo creator show, they are the same person, and the EP work is what gets dropped first when a recording session is staring back at the host.

## The eight functions of an EP

1. **Editorial vision.** What the show is about, what it never covers, and what point of view it carries.
2. **Format ownership.** Segment structure, runtime, recurring beats, cold open style, sign-off.
3. **Talent direction.** Briefing the host and co-host, shaping prep depth, calibrating energy and pacing.
4. **Production scheduling.** Topic pipeline, recording dates, publish dates, async handoffs.
5. **Quality control.** Reviewing the cut, the title, the description, the chapters, the thumbnail, the clips.
6. **Brand consistency.** Voice, tone, recurring phrases, banned phrases, visual identity.
7. **Distribution strategy.** Audio platforms, YouTube, short-form clips, newsletter tie-ins, paid amplification.
8. **Performance review.** Pulling listen and view data, reading comments, scoring episodes against goals, feeding lessons back into the next cycle.

## What a solo creator can hand to software

Most of the EP function space is structural — pulling sources, drafting documents, generating packaging copy, maintaining a calendar, parsing a transcript. A skill-based assistant can carry the load on:

- Scanning configured sources for episode-worthy material and ranking it against the show's audience
- Drafting a run-of-show that the host edits rather than writes from scratch
- Compiling fact-cited research briefs with working URLs
- Producing a separate co-host brief in the right framing for the second seat
- Generating titles, descriptions, chapters, takeaways, and a cleaned transcript from raw audio
- Surfacing 8–12 clip candidates per episode with timestamps and hook captions
- Drafting platform-specific distribution copy that respects the show's banned-word list
- Maintaining the editorial calendar and writing recording and publish dates to a shared calendar
- Pulling early performance data and writing a debrief that updates the calendar entry

## What still needs the host

Some EP work does not survive automation. A solo creator should expect to keep the call on:

- **Final topic selection.** Software can rank. Taste, timing, and the host's gut on what feels worth doing this week is the call.
- **Tough editorial decisions.** Anything legal-adjacent, anything personal, anything that could land badly with a sponsor or a guest. Read it yourself.
- **In-the-moment talent direction.** Pacing, energy, when to let the co-host run, when to cut a segment short.
- **Final clip pick.** A clip lives or dies on vibes. The model can hand you 12 candidates. You pick the ones that hit.
- **Brand-defining decisions.** Voice shifts, format changes, what the show stops covering. Keep these on the host's desk.
- **Final QC sign-off.** Listen to the cut before publish. No tool replaces this.

## The composable workflow

Each skill stands alone with sensible defaults, but they chain when run in order:

```
topic-radar → episode-program → research-brief
                              → cohost-brief
                              ↓
                          [recording]
                              ↓
                         [transcript]
                              ↓
              show-notes → clip-finder → distribution-pack
                              ↓
                       editorial-calendar
                              ↓
                       post-show-debrief
                              ↓
                       (feeds topic-radar)
```

The loop closes at debrief. Performance data updates the relevance scoring on the next topic-radar pass, so the show learns from itself.

## Format-specific notes for a StarTalk-style commentary show

The reference format here is a solo host carrying ~90% of the talk time, with a remote co-host throwing articles, earnings reports, and follow-up questions to drive pacing. Two implications shape how the skills are designed:

- **Briefs hand the host ammunition, not scripts.** Bullets, contrarian angles, surprising stats, names and quotes. The host responds extemporaneously and does not want paragraphs to read.
- **The co-host needs a different document.** The run-of-show is for the host. The co-host gets setup lines, throws, and follow-up questions framed for their seat. Same factual grounding, different artifact.

For interview-driven shows, narrative shows, and panel shows, the skill set adapts but several skills need rework. `CONTRIBUTING.md` covers those adaptations.
