Anthropic shipped Opus 5 on Tuesday. Everyone wrote about that.

Almost nobody wrote about the Sonnet 5 variant they shipped alongside it, priced 40% below the prior Sonnet.

Here's the read.

Frontier models are how labs stay in the AI Twitter conversation. If you're not on the bench leaderboard, the discourse forgets about you. So you need the flagship.

But the flagship doesn't drive the revenue. Enterprise workloads at scale don't run on Opus. They run on Sonnet. By dropping the Sonnet price 40% the same day Opus 5 launched, Anthropic is signaling two things:

1. They expect inference cost to keep collapsing — fast enough to underbid their own previous tier.
2. They want to lock in the volume layer before OpenAI's next move.

Dario Amodei said the line in his post-launch interview: "Models are moving from products to commodities." He said it four times. That's not a hedge. That's a signal.

If you're an enterprise buyer right now, the read is: don't lock in. There will be another aggressive pricing move from one of these labs in three months. Procurement decisions today should be optionality, not loyalty.

## Newsletter tie-in section (340 words)

Two pricing stories this week tell you something about where the model layer is going.

Anthropic launched Opus 5 on Tuesday. First model to clear 90% on the new LongBench-2 long-context benchmark. The headlines covered that part well. What the headlines mostly missed: Anthropic shipped Sonnet 5 the same day, priced 40% below the prior Sonnet tier. Forty percent.

The flagship model gets the press. The cheaper variant gets the revenue. Most enterprise workloads run on Sonnet, not Opus. By underbidding their own previous Sonnet by 40%, Anthropic is making a bet — that inference cost will keep falling fast, and that the company best positioned at the cheap-and-good tier will own the next eighteen months of enterprise volume. Dario Amodei said it explicitly in his post-launch interview: "models are moving from products to commodities." He said it four times.

The Spotify story rhymes. Q1 earnings showed podcast ad revenue up 28% YoY. Every write-up I read called it a comeback for the podcast ad business. The actual story is the programmatic stack — Spotify Audience Network API requests hit 4.2 billion in March, more than double a year ago. Advertisers are buying audio inventory at scale, the way they buy display. Spotify also wrote down $200M on a single exclusive deal this quarter and buried it in operating expenses. The exclusive-deal era is over. The routing era is starting. Daniel Ek said the line three times: "we're not in the content business, we're in the routing business."

Same shape. Two industries. The high-end product gets the attention while the volume play does the actual work, and the operators who notice the volume play move first get the next two years of margin.
